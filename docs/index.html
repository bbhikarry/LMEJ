Organising ourselves for Environmental Justice

In seeking to protect Nature to protect ourselves we are essentially fighting for rights that are not enshrined in the Constitution of Trinidad and Tobago. However there other issues to win that can sway our government to a better way;

We can use our NGO to build capacity and acquire capability to deal with Climate Change - that’s called Adaptation. Taking that road we will notice getting environmental justice is similar to other classes of lobby; it involves funding, research, mobilisation, reporting and scaling up.

As you read through this brief dear Invitee, hopefully you’ll take to the idea: Benefit the Lambeau community even as we gear up to tackle the bigger windmills.

“The February 7th oilspill off the southeast coast provides Environment Tobago with a teachable moment” [Latiff 2024]. Though at this point the only thing we have to work with are questions like; what is;

  • the impact on the fisherfolk whose livelihood is based in the locale,
  • the impact on the homeowners,
  • the impact on accommodation providers,
  • impact on the business community. Whose trade requires people visit Lambeau even if only for the purchase of a slice of fish, a sit down meal or simple enjoyment of seaside ambiance.

Knowledge of Impact is limited

All we know now is oil originated from a towed vessel (identified later as the MV Gulfstream) journeying from a point west; reportedly Puerto la Cruz, Venezuela and bound for Guyana. Satellite imagery provided by NOAA shows hydrocarbon leakage from 10 miles off Trinidad’s north coast. On February 7th 2024, four days afer leaving Venezuela waters the craft ran aground on a shallow reef off Cove Point, Tobago.

The aftermath of the grounding saw hydrocarbon pollution on a fifteen kilometer (15km) stretch of coast between Cove Point to the southeast and Lambeau to the northeast. The spillage continued for some time after grounding. Reports indicate the bulk of the spill post-grounding was carried to sea by current/wind and was tracked out to a distance of 180 kilometers further west. [Update: To Bonaire’s coast]

Over the period Feb 11th to time of writing, crews have been working with the oil and the vessel. Otherwise, except for some vouchers (100 x $1000TT) the only attention given to the village has been mandatory relocation of the fish market and an ‘offer’ to the Lambeau fishermen to take their boats to another unaffected bay.

The most recent news, other than the cleanup messaging delivered by the Tobago House of Assembly twice a week is that a donation of USD $250,000 is on its way to the country courtesy the CAF. This is a welcome start to the recovery but Environment Tobago would like to get involved ina meaningful way.

Is intervention still timely?

It is. Oil remains, killing the mangroves, it passed / is passing through the water column of the lagoon and will affect the near-shore (fish) nurseries. Is presently and possibly for the medium term prove deleterious to community assets (beach, fisheries, loss of human well-being etc). To reiterate, are all unknown states.

For restoration in any of these arenas to be successful more information has to gained. Environment Tobago thinks before leaving such works totally to the authorities and to the institutions charged with environmental and social responsibilities we’d do well to recall it is by someone’s failure to do a job that this situation is now with Tobago.

Moreover. The chances of other unplanned for events remain - as both Climate and geopolitical realities escalate toward unknown but definitely unpleasant territory. In short Trinidad and Tobago is located in a very risky part of the world and we who live here should look to scaling our capacities. As well as our rights.

So we should intervene. Incidentally Environment Tobago is already committed to bringing environmental rights awareness to this T&T via the EnviroRightsTT project (launching March 21st 2024). So while we acknowledge the unfortunate turn of events in the southwest, the Lambeau situation looks set to test of our ability both to assist and to grow in.
